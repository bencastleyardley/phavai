How Phavai Chooses Products

Phavai starts with a simple question: what would we recommend to a careful shopper, and what would we need to show to make that recommendation trustworthy?

Expert reviews: 40% Creator reviews: 30% Owner feedback: 30% Retail links never buy rank

1. Start with the buying problem

A good pick depends on the shopper and the category. Trail shoes need fit and terrain context. Office chairs need long-day comfort. Coffee makers need consistency and cleaning reality. The guide starts there.

2. Use category-specific sources

A specialist trail-running lab does not automatically become a trusted office-chair source. We weigh sources by how much they know about the category and how clearly they test the product.

3. Blend expert, creator, and owner evidence

Expert reviews set the strongest baseline. Creator reviews show real use, setup, fit, and annoyances. Owner feedback catches recurring praise, buyer regret, durability complaints, and support problems.

Channel formula: sum(evidence score x evidence quality weight) / sum(evidence quality weights).

4. Calculate the pick score

The default weighting is Expert 40%, Creator 30%, and Owner Feedback 30%. If a channel is missing, available channels are normalized rather than padded with a guess.

BestPick formula: sum(channel score x category channel weight) / sum(available category channel weights).

5. Show source agreement

Source agreement shows whether experts, creators, and owners are pointing in the same direction. A product can score well but still deserve caution if owners repeatedly flag fit, durability, or support issues.

6. Show evidence depth

Evidence depth reflects source count, source diversity, freshness, reviewer quality, sample strength, and channel depth. It is shown separately so thin evidence cannot hide behind a clean score.

7. Let readers tune the weighting

Some shoppers trust expert testing most. Others care more about owner feedback. Review pages let readers adjust the weighting in the browser without changing the underlying evidence.

8. Leave noisy social data out for now

Broad social media can be useful, but it is noisy and hard to audit cleanly. Phavai leaves it out until collection is reproducible enough to deserve influence.

9. Keep retail links separate

Retail links may point to Amazon, brand stores, or other retailers for convenience. They do not affect pick score, source agreement, evidence depth, ranking order, or product conclusions.

What makes a recommendation fail

A recommendation fails if it hides meaningful tradeoffs, uses unverifiable evidence, overstates certainty, or ranks a product because the link is more convenient. Trust is the product.
